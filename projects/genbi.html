The Problem

Business teams at Bank SinoPac needed data, but getting it meant going through analysts. Every ad-hoc question created a queue. Analysts spent a disproportionate amount of time on repetitive queries instead of higher-leverage work. The SQL bottleneck was real — and it was slowing down decisions.

The question I set out to answer: could an LLM reliably translate business questions into accurate SQL — not in a demo, but in a production banking environment with real schema complexity and financial data that couldn't afford to be wrong?

Architecture

I designed GenBI around a multi-stage pipeline. At the front end, a Streamlit chatbot interface collected user questions and routed them based on intent. The core engine used an LLM to generate SQL, which was executed directly against MSSQL. Results were returned as both structured data tables and auto-generated charts (bar, line, scatter), alongside a plain-language summary.

To ensure the model had the right context without stuffing the entire schema into every prompt, I had junior analysts and interns curate five years of customer segment insights and MSSQL pipeline syntax — a knowledge base that the agent could draw from before generating a response. The plan was to formalize this into a vector database (RAG layer), though I left the company before that phase was completed.

A feedback loop was built into the UI from the start: every user query and its output were logged to a database, along with thumbs-up / thumbs-down signals. This feedback was fed back into the agent's context — teaching it which query patterns worked, which failed, and which business logic assumptions were wrong.

System Architecture

What Was Shipped

By the time I left, the system could accept free-text questions through a chatbot interface, route them through the pipeline, and return a data table, chart, and plain-language summary — all in one view. Users could adjust chart type and axis selection. Every interaction was logged, and the thumbs-up / thumbs-down mechanism was live.

The knowledge base curation (business logic + schema context) was complete. The vector database layer that would have made retrieval more dynamic was scoped and designed, but not implemented before my departure.

What is the age distribution of digital banking customers? How does it compare to non-digital customers?
Answer
The bank's total customer base is approximately 10.83M. Digital banking customers account for 1.03M, representing a 9.5% digital penetration rate.

Digital customers are concentrated in the 25–44 age group, comprising 58% of the digital segment. Non-digital customers skew older, with 55+ accounting for 41% of that group.

Customers aged 65+ represent less than 3% of digital users — indicating significant growth potential in senior digital adoption.
Generated SQL Query
SELECT
  CASE
    WHEN f3k9_age < 25 THEN 'Under 25'
    WHEN f3k9_age BETWEEN 25 AND 34 THEN '25–34'
    WHEN f3k9_age BETWEEN 35 AND 44 THEN '35–44'
    WHEN f3k9_age BETWEEN 45 AND 54 THEN '45–54'
    WHEN f3k9_age BETWEEN 55 AND 64 THEN '55–64'
    ELSE '65+'
  END AS age_group,
  SUM(b7x2_digi_flag) AS digital_customers,
  COUNT(*) - SUM(b7x2_digi_flag) AS non_digital
FROM prd_dw.dbo.t8m4_cus_summary
WHERE (a1n5_save_mark >= 1
  OR c6p1_cc_mark >= 1)
GROUP BY age_group
ORDER BY digital_customers DESC;

Reflections

The 30% accuracy gap that remained was largely driven by schema ambiguity — cases where two columns could plausibly answer the same question. A retrieval layer trained on the curated knowledge base would have been the next meaningful lever. The feedback loop data we collected would have been the training signal.

Building this at a bank in early 2025 — before most teams had attempted it in production — was a forcing function for thinking through what "reliable enough" means when the data touches real customers and real decisions.
