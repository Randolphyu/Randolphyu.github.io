The Challenge

Credit card decline rates were higher than they should have been — and the cost was real. Every false decline is a lost transaction, a frustrated customer, and potential churn. But loosening the fraud detection thresholds without analysis risked increasing actual fraud losses.

The goal: reduce failure rates without increasing fraud cost. A constrained optimization problem, not just a model tuning exercise.

Analysis Approach

I specialized in the credit card and digital payment domain, analyzing decline and fraud patterns across transaction data in MSSQL. The core work was identifying which decline categories were false positives — legitimate transactions being blocked by overly conservative rules.

By segmenting declines by merchant category, transaction size, time of day, and customer history, patterns emerged: certain rule combinations were systematically over-triggering on low-risk transactions. The fraud signal and the false-positive signal had different statistical fingerprints.

Results

After re-calibrating the detection thresholds based on the analysis, monthly credit card failure rates dropped by 17%. Fraud cost remained flat — meaning the removed declines were indeed false positives, not fraud that slipped through.

What Made It Hard

Fraud modeling in banking requires navigating regulatory constraints and risk appetite discussions that go well beyond the data. The analytical work was only part of the job — communicating the tradeoffs clearly to risk and compliance stakeholders was equally important.
