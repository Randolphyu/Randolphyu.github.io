The Problem

Los Angeles is safer than only 6% of U.S. cities, with a violent crime rate twice the national average. The question we set out to answer was specific: given a location and time, what is the probability that a crime will occur? Not just a retrospective heatmap — an actual predictive model that could support proactive safety planning.

Spatial Structure — H3 Hexagonal Grid

Predicting individual incidents is intractable. Instead, each crime record is mapped to a H3 resolution-9 hexagon (~0.1 km² per cell) and aggregated by month and 3-hour time block. This produces a structured panel of 87,000+ grid-cell-hour observations — small enough to model, granular enough to be useful.

H3 Crime Hotspot Map — Los Angeles (2022–2023)
7,376 hexagons · resolution 9 · max 981 crimes per cell · crime concentrates around Downtown LA, Hollywood, and South LA

The map immediately confirms that crime is not uniformly distributed — it clusters in persistent hotspots driven by socioeconomic and environmental conditions. These spatial patterns became the foundation of the predictive features.

Data Engineering

The pipeline integrates four data sources, none of which arrive in a compatible format:

01LAPD Crime Data1,004,991 incidents · 28 columns · Socrata API · custom military-time parser (HHMM → HH:MM)
02ACS DemographicsCensus block group data via BigQuery · joined via two-stage geocoding: Census Geocoder API → OpenStreetMap fallback · 92% coverage (924k rows)
03NOAA WeatherDaily TAVG / TMAX / TMIN / PRCP for LA · merged by date · log-transformed COVID case counts to normalize scale
04Temporal Features1-month and 3-month lagged crime counts per grid · holiday indicator · season · time-of-day category

Modeling

The target variable is binary: does a grid-cell-hour fall in the top 20% of crime counts for its time period? This quantile-based labeling produces a ~2% positive rate — severe class imbalance that shaped every modeling decision.

XGBoost was selected for its 50.7% recall — correctly flagging half of all actual high-risk zones — and highest PR-AUC under class imbalance. Hyperparameters were tuned via GridSearchCV with TimeSeriesCV (5 splits) to prevent data leakage; random k-fold would leak future crime counts into training folds.

What Actually Drives Risk

SHAP analysis reveals a clear hierarchy: time of day dominates everything else. `time_block` has a mean absolute impact of +2.42 — nearly double the next feature. Risk escalates sharply after 3 PM and peaks between 6 PM and midnight.

SHAP Mean Absolute Impact — XGBoost
time_block (+2.42) and grid_max (+1.39) are the two dominant features — temporal context and historical crime density together explain most of the model's predictions

The second tier is historical crime density: `grid_max`, `grid_std`, and `hist_mean` collectively reflect the spatial stability of crime hotspots — areas that were dangerous before tend to remain dangerous. This aligns with environmental criminology theory: the physical and social conditions that create crime opportunity persist over time.

SHAP Waterfall — Single High-Risk Prediction (p = 0.979)
grid_mean (+1.44), hist_mean (+0.80), grid_std (+0.69) — the model is not a black box; every prediction can be traced to its drivers

The beeswarm confirms the directionality: high `time_block` values (late night) push predictions strongly negative for low-risk classifications, while high `grid_mean` values consistently drive risk up regardless of time.

What Made It Hard

The original panel structure — all grid × hour combinations — generated 90 million rows, exceeding memory limits. Reducing this to 87,000 records required careful aggregation choices that preserved predictive signal without leaking future information. Getting the cross-validation right under temporal dependency was the most technically demanding part: bootstrapping and standard k-fold both introduce subtle leakage that inflates performance estimates by 3–5%.
